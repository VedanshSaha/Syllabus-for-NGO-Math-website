# Grade 5: The Great Unification Begins
### A Complete Curriculum in Eight Units

This grade is where fractions, decimals, ratios, and early algebra start revealing themselves as expressions of the same underlying ideas.

---

## Unit 1: Place Value Across Decimals

**The Big Idea**
The base-ten bundling pattern doesn't stop at the decimal point — it simply runs in reverse below it. Each place to the right is one-tenth the size of the place before it, so a digit's value still depends entirely on its position.

**The Hook**
If 10 ones bundle into 1 ten, what happens if you go the other direction — what is one-tenth of a single "one"?

**Building the Idea**
Extend base-ten blocks below the "ones" cube: the same cube that represented "1" before now gets sliced into 10 equal flat pieces — each is "one tenth." Slice one of those again into 10 strips — each is "one hundredth." Build 3.45 physically: 3 ones-cubes, 4 tenths-pieces, 5 hundredths-strips. The rule "ones, tenths, hundredths" is the same bundling pattern as "hundreds, tens, ones," just continuing past the decimal point instead of stopping there.

**Worked Examples**
- Build 2.34 in expanded form: 2 + 0.3 + 0.04.
- Compare 0.7 and 0.65 by aligning decimal points and comparing tenths first: 7 tenths beats 6 tenths, regardless of the extra digit in 0.65.
- Round 4.378 to the nearest tenth by checking which tenth-mark it's closest to on a number line.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often assume more digits after the decimal point automatically means a bigger number — thinking 0.65 > 0.7 because "65" looks bigger than "7." Counter directly by aligning decimal points vertically and comparing place by place from the left, exactly as with whole numbers.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Compare 0.8 and 0.75, explaining with place value.
2. Build 1.23 using base-ten blocks or a drawing.
3. Round 6.84 to the nearest whole number.

**Why This Matters Later**
This "same rule, running in both directions from the decimal point" idea is exactly what makes scientific notation in Grade 8 feel like a continuation of something familiar, instead of a brand-new concept.

## Unit 2: Operations with Fractions

**The Big Idea**
Adding or subtracting fractions genuinely requires the same-sized "ruler" (denominator) for both fractions, and multiplying or dividing fractions can be reasoned through visually, rather than recalled as a disconnected "flip and multiply" rule.

**The Hook**
Why can you add 2 apples + 3 apples easily, but can't simply add 1/2 + 1/3 by just adding the tops and bottoms?

**Building the Idea**
Revisit common-denominator reasoning for addition and subtraction. For multiplication, use an area model: 1/2 × 1/3 is literally "half of a third," shown by shading a third of a rectangle, then shading half of that shaded region — the overlap is the answer (1/6). For division, use the "how many groups fit" idea: 1/2 ÷ 1/4 asks "how many quarters fit into a half," answered visually by overlaying fraction strips (the answer is 2).

**Worked Examples**
- 1/2 × 1/3 visualized as overlapping shaded regions = 1/6.
- 3/4 ÷ 1/2 reasoned as "how many halves fit in three-quarters," using fraction strips = 1.5.
- 2/3 + 1/4 using a common denominator: 8/12 + 3/12 = 11/12.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often apply the "flip and multiply" division rule mechanically, with no idea why it works, and confuse it with the multiplication rule. Ground every fraction-division problem in the "how many groups fit" picture first, so the shortcut procedure is never the *only* thing they know.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Find 2/5 × 1/2 using an area model.
2. Find 1/3 ÷ 1/6 using the "how many groups fit" reasoning.
3. Add 3/8 + 1/4 using a common denominator.

**Why This Matters Later**
The area-model reasoning for multiplying fractions becomes literally the same model used to multiply algebraic expressions like (x+1)(x+2) in Grade 8, and the "how many groups fit" idea for division previews unit rates in Grade 6.

## Unit 3: Operations with Decimals

**The Big Idea**
Decimal operations follow exactly the same logic as whole-number operations — you simply have to track where the decimal point sits, since it marks the boundary of the "ones" bundle size.

**The Hook**
If 23 + 45 is easy, why does 2.3 + 4.5 ever feel harder — what's actually different about it?

**Building the Idea**
Align decimal points vertically, just as place values are aligned in whole-number addition, so that tenths add to tenths and hundredths add to hundredths. For multiplication, connect to fraction multiplication from the prior unit: 0.2 × 0.3 is the same as 2/10 × 3/10 = 6/100 = 0.06 — so the "where does the decimal point go" rule has a real reason behind it, not just a digit-counting trick.

**Worked Examples**
- 2.35 + 1.7, aligned by decimal point, carrying as needed exactly like whole numbers.
- 0.4 × 0.2, reasoned through the fraction connection: 4/10 × 2/10 = 8/100 = 0.08.
- 3.6 ÷ 0.4, reasoned as "how many 0.4s fit into 3.6," by multiplying both numbers by 10 to make it 36÷4=9 without changing the answer.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often miscount decimal places when multiplying, or add mismatched-length decimals straight from the right edge as if they were whole numbers (e.g., adding 3.4 + 1.25 incorrectly). Insist on lining up by the decimal point, adding placeholder zeros when helpful: 3.40 + 1.25.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Add 5.6 + 2.45, aligning carefully.
2. Multiply 0.3 × 0.5, and check your answer against the fraction equivalent.
3. Divide 4.8 by 0.6, explaining your reasoning.

**Why This Matters Later**
This careful place-tracking is essential for unit conversions and scientific calculations throughout Grades 6–8, and the "multiply both by the same power of ten" division trick directly previews solving equations by scaling in algebra.

## Unit 4: Ratio & Proportional Thinking Begins

**The Big Idea**
A ratio compares two quantities by their relationship, not their raw difference — and recognizing when two situations share "the same relationship," even with totally different numbers, is the single most powerful new idea this year.

**The Hook**
A small batch of juice mix uses 2 cups of concentrate and 3 cups of water. If you wanted a much bigger batch that tastes exactly the same, would you add the same extra amount of water as concentrate — or something else?

**Building the Idea**
Build physical scaled-up batches (2:3 becomes 4:6, then 6:9) and discuss why these all "taste the same" even though the actual amounts differ — the relationship between the two quantities, not their size, is what's preserved. Connect explicitly to equivalent fractions: 2:3 is really "the same kind of split" as 4:6, just like 1/2 = 2/4.

**Worked Examples**
- Scale a recipe ratio of 2:3 up to a tripled batch (6:9), and verify it's still the same "taste ratio."
- Given a class ratio of boys to girls of 3:4 and a total of 28 students, recognize that 28 must split into 7 equal "ratio units" of 4 students each (3 units boys = 12, 4 units girls = 16).

**Misconception Alert**
Children often think doubling one quantity in a ratio means simply adding some fixed amount to the other, rather than scaling both proportionally. Use physical scaled batches to directly disprove this.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Scale a 3:5 ratio up to a total of 40, by finding how many "ratio units" fit.
2. Determine if 4:6 and 6:9 represent the same ratio, and explain how you checked.
3. A fruit punch recipe uses a 1:4 ratio of juice to water. How much juice and water are needed for a batch totaling 20 cups?

**Why This Matters Later**
This exact ratio reasoning becomes formal rates and proportional relationships in Grade 6, and graphing proportional relationships as straight lines through the origin in Grade 7.

## Unit 5: Volume & 3D Geometry

**The Big Idea**
Volume is simply "how many unit cubes fit inside a solid" — the same counting idea as area's "how many unit squares fit," just one dimension up — and the length×width×height formula is a fast way to count those cubes using the same array logic as multiplication.

**The Hook**
If area tells you how many flat unit squares cover a surface, what would tell you how many unit cubes fill up a box?

**Building the Idea**
Physically build a small rectangular box out of unit cubes (e.g., 3×2×2), and count the cubes layer by layer, discovering each layer is itself an "area" (3×2=6 cubes per layer), with 2 layers stacked, giving 12 total — directly connecting volume to the area idea from Grade 3, stacked one dimension higher.

**Worked Examples**
- Build a 4×3×2 box from unit cubes; count layer by layer (4×3=12 per layer, ×2 layers=24 total).
- Find the volume of an irregular solid made of two combined rectangular blocks, by finding each block's volume and adding.

**Misconception Alert**
Children sometimes think volume is found by adding length+width+height rather than multiplying, confusing it with perimeter-style thinking. Ground every volume problem in physically counting layers of cubes before ever applying the formula as a shortcut.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Build and find the volume of a 5×2×3 box using unit cubes.
2. Find the volume of an L-shaped solid made of two combined rectangular blocks.
3. Explain in your own words why volume is multiplied, not added.

**Why This Matters Later**
This layer-by-layer counting becomes the basis for surface area in Grade 6 (unfolding the same solid into flat nets), and is the direct three-dimensional extension of the array-and-area reasoning threaded through every earlier grade.

## Unit 6: Coordinate Plane Introduction

**The Big Idea**
A coordinate pair is just an address — two numbers that tell you exactly where a point lives by how far it is from two reference lines, exactly like giving directions using "blocks across, blocks up."

**The Hook**
If you wanted to tell a friend exactly where to find a specific point on a giant grid without pointing at it, what two pieces of information would be enough?

**Building the Idea**
Build a large floor or paper grid, and physically walk to points by following "go right 3, then up 2" instructions, before any formal (x,y) notation appears — the order (across first, then up) becomes a felt habit through movement, not an arbitrary memorized rule.

**Worked Examples**
- Plot the point (3,2) by walking 3 right and 2 up from the origin.
- Plot several points and connect them in order to reveal a simple shape.
- Identify the coordinates of a few given points already plotted on a grid.

**Misconception Alert**
Children frequently reverse the order, going up first and then across, especially once the physical "right, then up" habit fades into pure notation. Reinforce "x comes first, just like reading left to right," and revisit the physical walking activity if confusion returns.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Plot (1,4), (3,4), (3,1), (1,1) in order and connect them. What shape do you get?
2. Give a partner coordinates for a "mystery point" and have them find it on a grid.
3. Identify the coordinates of three points already marked on a grid.

**Why This Matters Later**
This exact (x,y) addressing system becomes the stage for graphing proportional relationships in Grade 6, linear equations in Grade 8, and the entire foundation of graphing functions throughout high school.

## Unit 7: Pre-Algebra — Expressions & Equations as Balance

**The Big Idea**
An equation is a balance scale frozen at a moment of perfect balance — whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other to keep it level, and "solving" an equation just means figuring out what unknown weight keeps the scale balanced.

**The Hook**
A balance scale has a mystery weight plus three known 2-gram weights on one side, perfectly balanced against a single known 10-gram weight on the other. How heavy is the mystery weight, and how do you know for sure?

**Building the Idea**
Use a real or drawn balance scale, with the unknown represented by a box or "x." Physically (or visually) remove the same weight from both sides to isolate the unknown, demonstrating that the scale stays balanced as long as both sides change equally — this *is* solving an equation, performed as an action rather than a memorized rule.

**Worked Examples**
- Solve x+3=10 by visualizing removing 3 grams from both sides, leaving x=7.
- Solve 2x=14 by visualizing splitting both sides into 2 equal balanced groups, leaving x=7.
- Write an expression for "5 more than a mystery number" as x+5, distinguishing an expression (no equals sign, no single answer) from an equation (has an equals sign, can be solved).

**Misconception Alert**
Children often confuse expressions and equations, trying to "solve" an expression like x+5 for a single number when there's nothing to balance it against. Explicitly distinguish the two using the balance-scale picture — an expression is one side of a scale with nothing yet on the other side.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Solve x+4=12 using the balance idea, explaining each step as an action on both sides.
2. Write an expression (not an equation) for "3 less than a mystery number."
3. Explain in your own words why doing something to only one side of an equation would "break" the balance.

**Why This Matters Later**
This "do the same thing to both sides" reasoning is the entire method used to solve every equation throughout Grades 6–8 and beyond — multi-step equations, equations with variables on both sides, and eventually systems of equations are all this same balance idea, just with more steps.

## Unit 8: Data Analysis & Line Plots

**The Big Idea**
A line plot shows every individual data point along a number line, making it possible to see the actual shape and spread of a dataset at a glance — something a single average number can hide entirely.

**The Hook**
Two classes both have an average test score of 75. Does that necessarily mean both classes' scores actually look similar?

**Building the Idea**
Build a line plot together from a real, small dataset (such as shoe sizes or heights of everyone in the group), placing a mark above the number line for every data point. Discuss what the plot's overall shape reveals — clusters, gaps, outliers — that a single average number alone cannot show.

**Worked Examples**
- Build a line plot from {3,3,4,4,4,5,5,6,8}, and discuss its shape: most values cluster around 4–5, with one unusual high value at 8.
- Compare two different datasets with the same mean but very different line-plot shapes — one tightly clustered, one widely spread.

**Misconception Alert**
Children sometimes treat the mean as a complete summary of a dataset, missing that very different-looking datasets can share the exact same average. Directly construct two contrasting datasets with matching means to make this vivid and concrete.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Collect a small real dataset (such as the number of siblings each person in your group has), and build a line plot from it.
2. Explain what the line plot's shape tells you that the average alone wouldn't.
3. Build two different datasets that both have a mean of 5, but look very different on a line plot.

**Why This Matters Later**
This attention to the "shape" of data beyond a single average directly previews measures of spread in Grade 6 statistics, and the comparison-of-distributions thinking used for sampling and comparing populations in Grade 7.
