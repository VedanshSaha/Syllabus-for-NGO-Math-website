# Grade 6: Crossing Into Algebra
### A Complete Curriculum in Eight Units

Middle school officially begins here: the number line extends in both directions, and letters stop being scary.

---

## Unit 1: Integers

**The Big Idea**
Negative numbers aren't "less than nothing" — they're numbers in the opposite direction on the number line, and thinking of them as direction (or debt) rather than a strange new category makes every operation with them predictable instead of mysterious.

**The Hook**
If you owe a friend 5 rupees, and then you owe them 3 more, how much do you now owe in total — and can you describe what happened using a single number that captures "owing," not just "having"?

**Building the Idea**
Extend the number line both directions from zero: movement to the right is positive, to the left is negative. Use a debt or elevator metaphor — a floor below ground level is "negative," and going down further from -2 to -5 is moving 3 floors deeper, the same kind of move as adding 3 in the positive direction, just mirrored.

**Worked Examples**
- -3 + 5: start at -3, move 5 steps right, land at +2.
- -2 - 3: start at -2, move 3 more steps left (subtracting moves further negative), land at -5.
- Compare -8 and -3 by checking which is further right on the number line: -3 is bigger, even though 8>3, because direction matters.

**Misconception Alert**
Children very reliably think -8 is "bigger" than -3 because 8>3 as plain numbers, ignoring that further left on the number line means smaller once you're below zero. Ground every comparison in the number-line picture, not just the digit.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Find -4 + 7 and -4 - 7 using a number line, explaining the direction of each move.
2. Order -10, -1, and -15 from smallest to largest, explaining your reasoning.
3. A submarine is at -120 m (120 m below sea level) and descends another 45 m. What's its new depth?

**Why This Matters Later**
This number-line direction model for integers is exactly what's needed to understand slope and rate of change in Grades 7–8, and operations with negative numbers here are essential for solving equations with negative solutions throughout algebra.

## Unit 2: Ratios, Rates & Proportional Reasoning

**The Big Idea**
A rate is a ratio comparing two different kinds of quantities (like distance and time), and a "unit rate" is that ratio scaled down to compare against just "1" of something — which is exactly why unit rates make comparing deals or speeds so much easier.

**The Hook**
One shop sells 4 mangoes for 60 rupees; another sells 7 mangoes for 98 rupees. Which shop actually has the better deal, and how can you compare them fairly when the quantities don't even match?

**Building the Idea**
Scale both ratios down to find the price "per single mango" — a unit rate — making the two deals directly comparable for the first time. This connects explicitly to the ratio-scaling work from Grade 5: finding a unit rate is just scaling a ratio down until one side becomes exactly 1.

**Worked Examples**
- 4 mangoes/60 rupees → 15 rupees/mango. 7 mangoes/98 rupees → 14 rupees/mango. The second shop is the better deal.
- A car covers 12 km in 3 hours. Scaled down: 4 km per 1 hour.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often assume the deal with bigger total numbers (98 rupees for 7 mangoes) must automatically be worse value than a smaller total (60 rupees for 4 mangoes), without actually scaling to compare fairly. Insist on finding a genuine unit rate before declaring any deal "better."

**Try It Yourself**
1. Find the better deal between 5 pens for 50 rupees and 8 pens for 72 rupees, by finding unit rates.
2. Find the unit rate (km per hour) for a car covering 180 km in 3 hours.
3. A recipe uses 250 g of flour for 4 servings. What's the unit rate of flour per serving?

**Why This Matters Later**
Unit rates are exactly the "slope" of a proportional relationship's graph in Grade 7, and this scaling reasoning is identical to what's needed for converting between measurement units throughout science and later math.

## Unit 3: Fractions, Decimals, Percentages — Same Idea, Three Costumes

**The Big Idea**
Fractions, decimals, and percentages are three different costumes for exactly the same underlying idea — a part compared to a whole — and moving fluently between all three, recognizing they're never actually different numbers, is one of the single most useful skills in all of middle school math.

**The Hook**
A shirt is on sale for "25% off." Someone says that's the same as being off by 1/4 of the price; someone else says it's the same as being off by 0.25 of the price. Are all three of these people actually saying the exact same thing?

**Building the Idea**
Build a single visual model — a 10×10 grid of 100 squares — and shade the same amount three different ways: as a fraction of the whole grid (1/4 shaded), as a decimal (0.25 of the grid), and as a percentage (25 out of 100 squares — literally what "percent" means: "per hundred"). Seeing all three labels point at the exact same shaded region is the entire lesson.

**Worked Examples**
- Convert 3/4 to a decimal (0.75) and a percentage (75%) using the same 100-square grid.
- Find 20% of 60 by recognizing 20% means 20/100, so 20/100 × 60 = 12.
- Convert 0.6 to a fraction (3/5) and a percentage (60%).

**Misconception Alert**
Children often treat fraction-to-decimal-to-percentage conversion as three separate memorized procedures, leading to errors like thinking 0.4 = 40% but mistakenly computing 0.4 = 4%. The 100-square grid model directly prevents this by making the underlying equivalence visible every time.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Shade a 100-square grid to represent 35%, then write the same value as a decimal and a fraction in lowest terms.
2. Find 15% of 80 using the "per hundred" reasoning.
3. Convert 7/20 to a percentage.

**Why This Matters Later**
This unification is the single biggest unlock for proportional relationships in Grade 7, and for percent-based problems (discounts, interest, tax) that appear constantly in real-world and exam math throughout the rest of school.

## Unit 4: Algebraic Expressions

**The Big Idea**
A variable isn't a scary letter — it's a mystery box standing in for a number we don't know yet (or a number that can change), and an algebraic expression is a recipe describing what to do to that mystery box, written compactly.

**The Hook**
If a mystery box could hold any number, and you wanted to describe "5 more than whatever's in the box," how would you write that down without knowing the box's contents?

**Building the Idea**
Directly connect to the function-machine and missing-number-box work from Grades 3–5 — the box is now formally called "x," the exact same idea with a new name and a more compact notation. Build expressions from real-world stories (e.g., "the cost of n notebooks at 15 rupees each" becomes 15n) before manipulating any symbols.

**Worked Examples**
- Translate "7 less than a number" into x−7 — not 7−x, a common reversal.
- Evaluate 3x+2 when x=4: substitute to get 3(4)+2=14.
- Simplify 2x+3x: both terms describe "x" amounts and combine into 5x, just like 2 apples + 3 apples = 5 apples.

**Misconception Alert**
Children very commonly reverse subtraction order, writing "7 less than a number" as 7−x instead of x−7. Ground every translation in a concrete real-world story — if you have x rupees and spend 7, you have x−7 left, not 7−x — to fix the order intuitively rather than by memorized rule.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Translate "3 more than twice a number" into an expression.
2. Evaluate 4x−1 when x=5.
3. Simplify 6y+2y−y.

**Why This Matters Later**
This fluency in building and simplifying expressions is the direct foundation for every equation solved in Grades 6–8, and "combining like terms" here is identical to the reasoning used in far more complex high school algebra expressions.

## Unit 5: Equations

**The Big Idea**
Solving an equation means finding the one number that makes both sides of a balance scale level — and every solving technique is "doing the same thing to both sides" to gradually undo whatever's been done to the unknown, until it stands alone.

**The Hook**
If 2x+3=11, what's the actual value of x, and how could you figure it out using the balance-scale idea from before, just with an extra step this time?

**Building the Idea**
Extend the balance-scale model from Grade 5 to two-step equations, performing "undo" operations strictly in the reverse order of how they were applied — undo addition or subtraction first, then undo multiplication or division — always performing the same action on both sides to preserve balance.

**Worked Examples**
- 2x+3=11: subtract 3 from both sides (2x=8), then divide both sides by 2 (x=4).
- 3(x+2)=15: distribute first (3x+6=15), then solve as before (3x=9, x=3).
- Check a solution by substituting it back into the original equation to confirm both sides truly balance.

**Misconception Alert**
Children sometimes perform operations on only one side, or in the wrong order (dividing before subtracting), breaking the balance. Insist on always checking the solution by substitution, building a self-correcting habit rather than relying on getting the procedure right the first time.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Solve 4x−5=11, showing each balanced step.
2. Solve 2(x−3)=10.
3. Check your solution to problem 1 by substituting it back into the original equation.

**Why This Matters Later**
This "undo step by step, keep both sides balanced" method scales directly to equations with variables on both sides, and eventually to systems of equations in Grade 8 — nothing fundamentally new is added, only more steps.

## Unit 6: Geometry — Area, Surface Area & Nets

**The Big Idea**
The surface area of a 3D solid is the total area of all its flat faces added together — and "unfolding" a solid into its flat net is the best way to see and calculate this, rather than memorizing a separate formula for every shape.

**The Hook**
If you wanted to know exactly how much wrapping paper is needed to cover a box completely, with no waste and no gaps, how would you figure that out?

**Building the Idea**
Physically unfold a cardboard box (or a net diagram) flat, revealing all its faces laid out as simple rectangles and squares. Find the area of each face using the area reasoning from Grade 3, then add them all together — surface area emerges as a sum of familiar, smaller area problems.

**Worked Examples**
- Unfold a rectangular box net into 6 rectangular faces, find each face's area, and sum them for total surface area.
- Unfold a triangular prism's net into 2 triangles and 3 rectangles, finding and summing each piece's area.

**Misconception Alert**
Children sometimes confuse surface area with volume. Explicitly contrast the two using the same solid: volume asks "how much fits inside" (Grade 5's unit-cube counting), while surface area asks "how much covers the outside" (this unit's unfolded-face counting) — fundamentally different questions about the same object.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Find the surface area of a cube with 4 cm sides.
2. Unfold a rectangular box net and label each face with its dimensions and area.
3. Explain, in your own words, the difference between volume and surface area using one example solid.

**Why This Matters Later**
This "unfold into flat, familiar pieces" strategy is exactly how surface area and volume problems are approached in Grades 7–8 geometry, and tracking multiple smaller areas summing into a total previews structured, multi-step algebraic reasoning.

## Unit 7: Statistics — Measures of Center & Spread

**The Big Idea**
Mean, median, and mode are three different ways of answering "what's typical here?" — and each one can tell a different story about the same dataset, especially when extreme values are involved, so choosing the right one matters as much as calculating it correctly.

**The Hook**
A small company has 9 employees earning around 30,000 rupees each, and 1 owner earning 500,000 rupees. What's the "typical" salary at this company — and does the answer change depending on which measure of "typical" you choose?

**Building the Idea**
Calculate mean, median, and mode for the same dataset, directly comparing how differently they respond to an extreme value — the owner's salary drags the mean far higher than what almost everyone actually earns, while the median stays close to the real "typical" worker's pay. Introduce range as a first measure of spread, showing how far the data stretches.

**Worked Examples**
- For the salary dataset above, the mean is heavily skewed upward by the outlier, while the median is much more representative of a typical employee.
- For {2,4,4,5,9}: mean=4.8, median=4, mode=4 — close together, since there's no extreme value.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often assume the mean is always the "best" or "correct" measure of typical, not realizing it can be badly distorted by outliers. Deliberately construct datasets with and without extreme values to show why median is sometimes the more honest choice.

**Try It Yourself**
1. For {3,3,3,3,100}, calculate the mean and median, and explain which better represents what's "typical" here.
2. Find the range of {12,45,3,78,22}.
3. Explain, using the salary example, why "average income" headlines can sometimes be misleading.

**Why This Matters Later**
This critical awareness of how outliers distort the mean directly underlies the sampling and population-comparison reasoning in Grade 7, and is essential for correctly interpreting real-world statistics throughout life.

## Unit 8: Intro to Probability

**The Big Idea**
The probability of an event is a fraction — the number of ways that event can happen, divided by the total number of equally likely outcomes — turning the "likely/unlikely" intuition from Grade 3 into something calculable and precisely comparable.

**The Hook**
If you roll a regular 6-sided die, what's the exact probability of rolling an even number — and how confident can you be in that number before ever rolling it once?

**Building the Idea**
Connect directly to Grade 3's counting-based probability intuition, now formalized as a fraction: probability = (favorable outcomes) ÷ (total possible outcomes). Verify theoretical probability against real experimental results by rolling dice or drawing from a bag many times, comparing the experimental fraction to the calculated theoretical one.

**Worked Examples**
- Probability of rolling an even number on a die: 3 favorable outcomes out of 6 total = 1/2.
- Probability of drawing a red marble from a bag of 4 red and 6 blue: 4/10 = 2/5.
- Roll a die 30 times, tally results, and compare the experimental probability of rolling a 6 to the theoretical 1/6.

**Misconception Alert**
Children sometimes expect experimental results to exactly match theoretical probability every time (assuming a fair coin flipped 10 times must give exactly 5 heads). Use real repeated trials to show experimental results vary, especially with small numbers of trials, while still trending toward the theoretical probability as trials increase.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Find the probability of drawing a king from a standard deck of cards.
2. Roll a die 20 times and compare your experimental probability of rolling an odd number to the theoretical probability.
3. Explain why getting 7 heads out of 10 coin flips doesn't mean the coin is unfair.

**Why This Matters Later**
This fraction-based probability becomes the basis for compound and simulated probability involving multiple events in Grade 7, and the experimental-vs-theoretical comparison here builds the statistical reasoning needed for sampling in Grade 7 as well.
