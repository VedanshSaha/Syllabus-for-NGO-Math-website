# Grade 7: Reasoning Gets Formal
### A Complete Curriculum in Seven Units

This grade unifies all rational numbers, formalizes proportional relationships, and starts proving geometric facts instead of just stating them.

---

## Unit 1: Rational Numbers Unified

**The Big Idea**
Integers, fractions, and decimals — positive or negative — are all just members of one single family called rational numbers, and the rules for adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing them are the exact same rules learned separately in earlier grades, now working together at once.

**The Hook**
If you can add positive fractions, and you can add negative integers, can you also handle something like -3/4 + 1.5, a mixture of both worlds at once?

**Building the Idea**
Revisit the number-line direction model from Grade 6's integers and the equivalence model from Grade 6's fractions/decimals/percentages unit, and combine them: every rational number, no matter its costume, has one exact location on the same number line, and operations work identically everywhere on that line.

**Worked Examples**
- -3/4 + 1.5: convert 1.5 to 6/4, so -3/4 + 6/4 = 3/4.
- -2.5 × 4: use the familiar "negative times positive is negative" rule from integers, now applied to a decimal, giving -10.
- Order -1.2, -1/2, and 0.3 on a number line by converting all three to a common form first.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often treat negative fractions and negative decimals as needing entirely separate rule sets, rather than recognizing they're governed by the same sign rules already learned for integers. Point out, every time, that the sign rule never changes — only the "size" part of the number does.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Compute -5/6 − 1/3.
2. Compute -0.4 × -5.
3. Order -3, -1/4, and -2.5 from smallest to largest.

**Why This Matters Later**
This unification is exactly what's needed to comfortably handle the "real number system" framing in Grade 8, and operating fluently with all rational numbers at once is essential for solving multi-step equations involving mixed number types.

## Unit 2: Proportional Relationships & Their Graphs

**The Big Idea**
A proportional relationship is one where two quantities always scale together by the same constant ratio — and when graphed, it always produces a straight line through the origin, with the line's steepness (slope) being exactly the unit rate from Grade 6, now seen visually.

**The Hook**
If a car travels at a steady 60 km/h, and you plotted distance against time on a graph, what shape would you expect — and why would it have to pass through the very origin point (0,0)?

**Building the Idea**
Build a table of values for a real proportional situation (distance = speed × time), plot the points on a coordinate plane, and observe they always form a straight line through the origin. Connect the line's steepness explicitly to the unit rate calculated numerically in Grade 6 — "how much y changes per step of x" is the same number as the rate "per 1" from before.

**Worked Examples**
- Graph y=3x (e.g., cost = 3 × number of items) by plotting (1,3), (2,6), (3,9), and observe the constant ratio y/x=3 matches the line's steepness.
- Determine if a given graphed line represents a proportional relationship, by checking whether it passes through the origin and stays straight.

**Misconception Alert**
Children sometimes assume any straight line represents a proportional relationship, missing that a line which doesn't pass through the origin (like one with a starting fee plus a per-unit cost) is *not* proportional, even though it's still straight. Directly contrast a proportional line through the origin with a shifted, non-proportional line.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Graph y=2x and find its steepness by comparing rise to run between two points.
2. Determine whether "cost = 5 + 2×(number of items)" is proportional, and explain why or why not using a graph.
3. Find the unit rate represented by the graph of y=4x.

**Why This Matters Later**
This line-through-the-origin idea becomes a special case of the linear functions formally explored in Grade 8 (where a "starting fee" reappears as the y-intercept), and slope as "steepness" here is the literal definition of slope used throughout algebra.

## Unit 3: Multi-Step Expressions & Equations

**The Big Idea**
Multi-step equations and expressions with the distributive property aren't a fundamentally new challenge — they're the same balance-scale and "fair sharing of multiplication" ideas from earlier grades, just combined and applied across a longer sequence of steps.

**The Hook**
If 3 friends are each charged the same delivery fee plus the same per-item price, and their total bills differ because they ordered different numbers of items, how could you write one single expression describing everyone's bill?

**Building the Idea**
Build multi-step expressions directly from real situations (total cost = flat fee + price × quantity), explicitly revisiting the distributive property from Grade 3's "break it apart" strategy — now run in reverse to expand expressions like 3(x+4) into 3x+12, and forward to factor expressions like 3x+12 back into 3(x+4).

**Worked Examples**
- Expand 3(x+4): 3×x + 3×4 = 3x+12. Verify by substituting a number for x into both forms.
- Solve 3(x+4)=21: expand first (3x+12=21), then balance (3x=9, x=3).
- Factor 5x+15 into 5(x+3) by recognizing the shared factor of 5 in both terms.

**Misconception Alert**
Children commonly forget to distribute to every term inside parentheses, multiplying only the first term (computing 3(x+4) as 3x+4 instead of 3x+12). Insist on drawing an arrow from the outside multiplier to each term inside the parentheses, every time, until the habit is automatic.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Expand 4(x+5), and check your answer by substituting x=2 into both the expanded and unexpanded forms.
2. Solve 2(x−3)+4=10.
3. Factor 6x+18 by finding the shared factor.

**Why This Matters Later**
This fluency in expanding and factoring is the exact skill needed to multiply algebraic binomials and factor quadratic expressions in Grade 8 and high school algebra — nothing new is added beyond more terms to track.

## Unit 4: Inequalities

**The Big Idea**
Not every real-world question has a single exact answer — some questions ("how many can I afford?", "how fast is too fast?") have a whole range of correct answers, and an inequality describes that entire range at once, instead of pretending there's only one right number.

**The Hook**
If you have 50 rupees and want to buy notebooks costing 8 rupees each, what's the most notebooks you could buy — and is there really only one correct answer, or a whole range of acceptable ones (0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6)?

**Building the Idea**
Connect directly to the equation-balancing skills from Grade 6, with one crucial new wrinkle: solving an inequality follows the same balanced steps as an equation, except multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number flips the inequality's direction. Demonstrate this concretely: test 2<5, multiply both sides by -1, and notice -2<-5 is false unless the sign flips to -2>-5, which is true.

**Worked Examples**
- 8x ≤ 50: divide both sides by 8 (x ≤ 6.25), interpreted as "at most 6 notebooks," since a fractional notebook isn't possible.
- -2x < 10: divide both sides by -2 and flip the inequality, giving x > -5.
- Graph the solution to x>3 on a number line, using an open circle and shading.

**Misconception Alert**
Children very commonly forget to flip the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative number, treating inequalities exactly like equations in every respect. Always test the final answer with a real number to verify the inequality still makes sense.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Solve 3x+2 > 11.
2. Solve -4x ≥ 20, remembering to consider whether the sign needs to flip.
3. Graph the solution to x ≤ -2 on a number line.

**Why This Matters Later**
This "range of answers" thinking, along with the careful sign-flipping rule, is essential for systems of inequalities and optimization problems throughout Grade 8 and high school algebra.

## Unit 5: Geometry — Angles, Triangles, Circles

**The Big Idea**
Many geometric facts that feel like they must simply be memorized — like "a triangle's angles always sum to 180°," or the formulas for a circle's circumference and area — can be discovered and even proven yourself with hands-on investigation, rather than taken purely on faith.

**The Hook**
Why do a triangle's three angles always add up to exactly 180°, no matter how stretched, squished, or oddly shaped the triangle is?

**Building the Idea**
Tear off the three corners of any paper triangle and physically rearrange them, edge to edge, around a single point — they always form a perfectly straight line (180°), no matter the triangle's original shape, directly proving the angle-sum rule through hands-on evidence. For circles, wrap a string around several different circular objects and measure the string length (circumference) against each circle's diameter, discovering the ratio is always approximately 3.14 (π), no matter the circle's size — this is literally how π is reasoned into existence, not handed down as a mystery number.

**Worked Examples**
- Tear and rearrange a scalene triangle's corners to confirm 180°, then find a missing angle when two angles are known (50°+70°+?=180°, so ?=60°).
- Measure circumference and diameter for three circular objects, calculate circumference÷diameter for each, and notice all three cluster around 3.14.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often treat π as an arbitrary, mysterious number to plug into a formula, with no sense of where it comes from. The string-and-measure activity directly demystifies it, letting children discover the ratio for themselves before ever being told its name.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Tear and rearrange the corners of a triangle you draw yourself, and confirm the angle sum.
2. Find the missing angle in a triangle with known angles of 35° and 95°.
3. Measure a circular object's circumference and diameter, and calculate their ratio.

**Why This Matters Later**
This proof-by-physical-evidence approach to triangle angles becomes the literal foundation of the Pythagorean theorem derivation in Grade 8, and discovering π through measurement makes every later circle-area calculation feel earned rather than arbitrary.

## Unit 6: Probability — Compound Events & Simulation

**The Big Idea**
When two or more chance events happen together, the probability of a specific combined outcome can be found by multiplying the individual probabilities — and building an actual list or tree of every possible combined outcome makes this multiplication rule visible and provable, rather than just a formula to apply blindly.

**The Hook**
If you flip a coin and then roll a die, what's the probability of getting heads AND rolling a 6 — and how would you even begin counting all the possible combined outcomes?

**Building the Idea**
Build a complete tree diagram or organized list of every possible outcome for two combined events (2 coin outcomes × 6 die outcomes = 12 total), count how many match the desired result, and compare that count-based probability to the result of simply multiplying the two individual probabilities (1/2 × 1/6 = 1/12) — discovering they always match.

**Worked Examples**
- Tree diagram for flipping a coin then rolling a die: 12 total outcomes, exactly 1 of them "heads and 6."
- Probability of drawing 2 red marbles in a row, without replacement, from a bag of 3 red and 2 blue: 3/5 × 2/4 = 3/10.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often forget that probabilities change when drawing without replacement, assuming the second draw has the same probability as the first. Explicitly recount the bag's new contents after each simulated draw to make the changing probability concrete.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Build a tree diagram for flipping a coin twice, and find the probability of getting two heads in a row.
2. Find the probability of drawing 2 blue marbles in a row, without replacement, from a bag of 4 blue and 6 red marbles.
3. Explain why the second draw's probability changes when marbles aren't replaced.

**Why This Matters Later**
This systematic counting of combined outcomes is the exact reasoning behind more advanced probability and combinatorics in high school, and the "does the probability change after each step" question previews conditional probability.

## Unit 7: Statistics — Sampling & Comparing Groups

**The Big Idea**
You almost never have data from an entire population — you have a sample, and good statistics is about collecting a sample that fairly represents the whole group, then honestly comparing different groups' typical values and spread, rather than just their raw totals.

**The Hook**
If you wanted to know the most popular subject among all students in your entire city, would it make sense to only ask the students at one single school? Why or why not?

**Building the Idea**
Contrast a biased sample (only asking kids at a math club about their favorite subject) with a more representative one (asking a genuinely mixed group), discussing why the biased sample gives a misleading picture. Then compare two genuine datasets (such as plant growth under two different conditions) using both center (from Grade 6) and spread, to fairly judge whether there's a meaningful difference, not just a difference in raw numbers.

**Worked Examples**
- Identify why surveying only people leaving a movie theater about "favorite type of movie" would produce a biased sample.
- Compare two small datasets of plant heights grown under different light conditions, calculating mean and range for each, and discussing whether the difference seems meaningful given the spread within each group.

**Misconception Alert**
Children often assume any difference between two groups' averages must be "real" and meaningful, without considering whether the natural spread within each group is large enough to make that difference unremarkable. Directly compare two datasets where means differ only slightly but internal spread is huge, to show why spread matters as much as the average itself.

**Try It Yourself**
1. Describe why asking only your close friends about their favorite sport would create a biased sample of "favorite sports among students."
2. Given two small datasets of test scores from two different study methods, compare their means and ranges, and discuss whether the difference looks meaningful.
3. Suggest a fairer way to sample opinions across an entire school than just asking your own class.

**Why This Matters Later**
This sampling and group-comparison reasoning is essential for understanding statistics responsibly throughout the rest of school and life, and underlies how real scientific studies and survey results should be critically read and questioned.
